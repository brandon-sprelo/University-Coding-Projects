4. Performance of Players

#new statistics
Missed_FG = dat$FGA - dat$FGM
Missed_FT = dat$FTA - dat$FTM 
PTS = dat$Points3 * 3 + dat$Points2 * 2  + dat$FTM
REB = dat$OREB + dat$DREB
MPG = dat$MIN/ dat$GP


#add columns
dat <- cbind(dat, Missed_FG)
dat <- cbind(dat, Missed_FT)
dat <- cbind(dat, PTS)
dat <- cbind(dat, REB)
dat <- cbind(dat, MPG)
EFF = ((dat$PTS + dat$REB + dat$AST + dat$STL + dat$BLK - dat$Missed_FG - dat$Missed_FT - dat$TO) / dat$GP)

5. Efficiency and Salary

#plotting EFF (x) and Salary (y), include lowess smooth line, compute linear correlation coeffecient 

mil_salary <- dat$Salary/1000000

plot(dat$EFF, mil_salary, main = "Scatterplot of Efficiency and Salary", xlab = 'Efficiency', ylab = 'Salary (in millions)')

cor(dat$EFF, mil_salary)
## [1] 0.655624
lines(lowess(dat$EFF, mil_salary), col= 'red')
text(30, 30, 'lowess', col = 'red')

What can you say about the relationship between Efficiency and Salary? The relationship between Efficiency and Salary is that if efficiency goes up, salary is most likely to follow. The correlation is 0.655 which indicates they are positively correlated.

#scatterplot for MPG > 20
players2 <- dat[dat$MPG > 20, ]

What can you say about the relationship between these two variables for the “more established players”? With MPG > 20 taken into account, the correlation is less than the relationship between efficency and salary of all players. However it is still positive which indicates a positive relationship between the two. If efficiency goes up, salary is likely to go up too. I think that there is a difference in correlation between the two because players with more experience’s payrates do not fluctuate as much as the total with rookies.
